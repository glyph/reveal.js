“A Good Technical Talk”

Content First

In this talk I’m going to discuss presentation style and rhetorical gimmickery a lot, because those are features that are common to all talks, technical or not. I’m not going to talk a lot about tehcnical content, because that’s unique to your particular talk. However, your technical content is the important bit. Don’t get wrapped up in any of the advice that I’m giving and think of it as gospel, because if your technical content is not good, your technical audience will notice that.

No Pressure

All that stuff about technical content is usually true, of course, except for in this talk; since I’m ostensibly giving a talk about how to be entertaining and compelling while giving a talk, if I’m not entertaining and compelling then I’m going to be missing the whole point. However, again, I stress that this is not true of the average technical talk: if you focus on the content first and make sure you have something meaningful to say, your presentation doesn’t have to be perfect.

But...

The fact that an imperfect presentation is OK does not mean that a crappy presentation is OK. So I’m going to talk a lot about what makes the average presentation crappy, and steps that you can take to avoid those common pitfalls. If you do successfully avoid these common pitfalls, you'll be way ahead of the game. A lot of presentational style is mindlessly copy/pasted from the speaker's previous experience watching people speak, so antipatterns propagate throughout a majority of technical talks. Once you really start paying attention to what works and doesn't work, you can craft a much more engaging than average - at least the "talk at a meetup or conference" average - without all that much effort.

Step 0:

Relax

(Rehearse)

The most important thing that you need to do when giving a any kind of talk is (click) to be relaxed. You should test your laptop with a bunch of different projectors so you're not worried about it breaking. You should show up with a backup copy of your slides on a USB key in case your laptop doesn't work at all. I'm going to be a bit heretical here - most people who give public-speaking advice will tell you that rehearsing (click) is super super important so you can deliver your talk well - and say that if you know a technical topic well, you can probably wing it. Rehearsing is more a means to an end, and the end is making sure that when you take the stage, you are composed and sure of yourself. Being nervous while giving a talk is a negative feedback loop: you're nervous, so you make mistakes, so you get more nervous, so you make more mistakes.

Step 1:

Introduce Yourself

Credential Yourself

The first thing you should do when you're delivering a technical talk is, briefly, (click) introduce yourself, and as part of doing so (click) establish why you should be talking about the subject you’re talking about. There’s a reluctance in technical circles to describe your qualifications in terms of academic degrees, job titles, or high-profile project experience, because that sounds like an argument from authority, which we all know, is a fallacy, and we all want our ideas to stand on their own merits. However, if you don’t give some indication of your credentials, then your audience will be distracted towards the beginning of your talk as they try to put your words into context. It’s fine if you don’t have any specific credentials on the topic that you’re discussing too - by laying that out right at the beginning, you’re letting the more experienced members of the audience know that it’s OK to give you feedback at the end.

Step 2: Summary

  • Don't put bullets on your slides.
  • Don't read the text from your screen.
  • Don't put the same text on your slides that you're going to read.
  • Keep in mind slides are a visual aid; keep them interesting.

Step 3

<code>

Most of the challenges I've just discussed are fairly universal to any kind of presentation. However, the display and discussion of code presents unique challenges for technical presentations.

Make Code
Samples Short

def write(example):
    while example.has_unnecessary_lines():
        example.delete_those_liens()
It's hard enough to follow code when you have all the time in the world to sit at your desk and bash your head against it.

Use A
Big Font

def write(example):
    while example.has_unnecessary_lines():
        example.delete_those_liens()
It doesn't cost any extra to use a big font in your code samples, but from some of the tiny, tiny code samples I've seen up on projectors, you'd think it does. Scale up your code samples until they're at the maximum width of a slide.

Test
Your Code

def write(example):
    while example.has_unnecessary_lines():
        example.delete_those_lines()
As you revise your presentation, you're going to revise your code. As you revise your code, you're going to introduce errors. Make sure that you have a some way to test your code.

Use
Emphasis

def write(example):
    while example.has_unnecessary_lines():
        example.delete_those_lines()
When you're discussing a piece of code, even one you've aggressively trimmed to fit on a slide, you will generally need to move from one portion of it to another. It's important that your audience be able to follow which piece exactly you're talking about. You can do this with a laser pointer, but (click) it's better if you embed this information into the slides.

The Audience
Isn’t Looking
At Your Laptop

The important principle to remember about slides with code on them is that your audience isn't looking over your shoulder at your laptop screen. They're looking at a blurry projector 25 feet across the room.

The Audience Doesn’t
Have Your Code
In An Editor

Your audience also doesn't have your code open in an editor. They're not running it in a terminal. They have to keep the entire state of the code in their brain.
One trick that you can use to help keep this in mind is to close your eyes and try to imagine re-constructing your whole example from scratch. Maybe even try typing it with your eyes closed. If you - the person who is familiar enough with this material to write a talk about it - can't easily do this in your head, then how do you expect your audience, who is new to the material, to do it? Also, as this diagram shows, you should use syntax highlighting in your presentation, as various syntax colors activate different regions in the brain.
