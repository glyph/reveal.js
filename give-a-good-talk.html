“A Good Technical Talk”

Content First

In this talk I’m going to discuss presentation style and rhetorical gimmickery a lot, because those are features that are common to all talks, technical or not. I’m not going to talk a lot about tehcnical content, because that’s unique to your particular talk. However, your technical content is the important bit. Don’t get wrapped up in any of the advice that I’m giving and think of it as gospel, because if your technical content is not good, your technical audience will notice that.

No Pressure

All that stuff about technical content is usually true, of course, except for in this talk; since I’m ostensibly giving a talk about how to be entertaining and compelling while giving a talk, if I’m not entertaining and compelling then I’m going to be missing the whole point. However, again, I stress that this is not true of the average technical talk: if you focus on the content first and make sure you have something meaningful to say, your presentation doesn’t have to be perfect.

But...

The fact that an imperfect presentation is OK does not mean that a crappy presentation is OK. So I’m going to talk a lot about what makes the average presentation crappy, and steps that you can take to avoid those common pitfalls. If you do avoid these common pitfalls

Step 1:

Introduce Yourself

Credential Yourself

The first thing you should do in any technical talk is, briefly, (click) introduce yourself, and as part of doing so (click) establish why you should be talking about the subject you’re talking about. There’s a reluctance in technical circles to describe your qualifications in terms of academic degrees, job titles, or high-profile project experience, because that sounds like an argument from authority, which we all know, is a fallacy, and we all want our ideas to stand on their own merits. However, if you don’t give some indication of your credentials, then your audience will be distracted towards the beginning of your talk as they try to put your words into context. It’s fine if you don’t have any specific credentials on the topic that you’re discussing too - by laying that out right at the beginning, you’re letting the more experienced members of the audience know that it’s OK to give you feedback at the end.

For pretty much all of us, technical presenting is at best an extracurricular activity, and at worst something we have to do entirely outside of work.
